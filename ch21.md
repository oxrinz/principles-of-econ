- Marginal rate of substitution = the rate at which a consumer is willing to trade one good for another
- When the goods are easy to substitute for each other, the indifference curves are less bowed; when the goods are hard to substitute, the indifference curves are very bowed
- In the case of perfect substitutes, the indifference curve is straight
- In the case of perfect complements, the indifference curve is right-angle
- The consumer chooses consumption of two goods so that the marginal rate of substitution equals the relative price
- A big enough price drop of a substitute of product x, no matter how big or small the substitute is, can cause the product x's consumtion to fall
- The income effect is the change in consumption that results from the movement to a higher indifference curve
- The substitution effect is the change in consumption that results from being at a point on an indifference curve with a different marginal rate of substitution
- A Giffen good is a good that violates the law of demand. That is, the demand rises as price rises. Giffen goods are inferior goods for which the income effect dominates the substitution effect, therefore, its demand curve slopes upward. This happens so rarely, if ever, that the law of demand is as reliable a law as any in economics. One potential example of a Giffen good is potatos during the Irish potato famine of the nineteenth century

**A person who consumes wine and cheese gets a raise, so his income increases from 3000€ to 4000€. Show what happens if both wine and cheese are normal goods. Now show what happens if cheese is an inferior good.**
If both are normal goods, the consumption of both will increase by x. If cheese is an inferior good, its consumption will decrease, and consequtively, consumption of wine will increase. However, wine's new consumption will be higher than x, because the person now has more capital to allocate across the options that are not cheese.
