**What happens to consumer and producer surplus when the sale of a good is taxed? How does the change in consumer and producer surplus compare to the tax revenue? Explain**
They shrink. As the market shrinks because of taxes, that lost market size is "lost" money. The government gets less money than the seller / buyers lose out on. This is called deadweight loss.

**How do the elasticities of supply and demand affect the deadweight loss of a tax? Why do they have this effect?**
The deadweight loss scales with elasticity. If the elasticiy of a market is 0, the market doesn't get any smaller from the taxes, and therefore the government gets the exact same amount of money that buyers / sellers pay. In elastic markets however, more of the market has to be cut off in order to meet the supply and demand, resulting in higher deadweight loss.

**Why do experts disagree about whether labor taxes have small or large deadweight losses?**
As noted in the previous answer, the deadweight loss depends on the elasticity of the market. Economists cannot agree on whether the labor market is elastic or not, since there are many immeasurable factors that go into it.

**What happens to the deadweight loss and tax revenue when a tax is increased?**
The deadweight loss scales exponentially with tax rates.
